Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about home care

In-home care (also called home care) provides non-medical assistance to seniors and adults in their own homes. Services typically include help with daily activities like bathing, dressing, meal preparation, medication reminders, light housekeeping, and companionship. Unlike home health care, in-home care does not require medical licensing and focuses on helping people maintain independence and quality of life.
Home care costs vary significantly by location, level of care needed, and hours required. The national average is approximately $25-35 per hour. Live-in care typically costs $200-400 per day. Costs tend to be higher in metropolitan areas and for specialized care like dementia support. Many agencies offer free consultations to provide personalized cost estimates based on your specific needs.
Medicare generally does not cover non-medical home care services like bathing assistance, meal preparation, or companionship. However, Medicare does cover home health care (skilled nursing, physical therapy) when prescribed by a doctor for homebound patients. Medicaid may cover some home care services depending on your state's programs. Other payment options include long-term care insurance and VA benefits for veterans.
Home care (non-medical) provides assistance with daily living activities like bathing, dressing, meal preparation, medication reminders, light housekeeping, and companionship. Home health care (medical) provides skilled nursing services, wound care, physical therapy, and other medical treatments prescribed by a doctor. Home care helps maintain independence and quality of life; home health care addresses specific medical conditions and recovery needs.
Look for agencies that are licensed in your state, conduct thorough background checks on caregivers, provide ongoing training and supervision, and have positive reviews from families. Ask about their caregiver matching process, backup coverage policies, and how they handle concerns or complaints. Request references from current clients and arrange to meet potential caregivers before services begin. Accreditations from organizations like the Joint Commission or CHAP indicate quality standards.

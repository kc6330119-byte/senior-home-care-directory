What this directory is — and isn't.

We're a simple, independent listing of in-home care agencies. Families find us through search, use the directory to compare options, and call the agencies directly. That's the whole product.

  • Agencies don't pay to appear here. There's no "featured" tier you can buy.
  • We don't sell your information or route calls through a middleman.
  • We're not a medical-advice site. For care recommendations, talk to a doctor.
  • We're not a substitute for checking an agency's license and references yourself.
Common questions

Before you start calling agencies.

Plain answers to the questions families ask us most.

In-home care, also called home care, is non-medical help for seniors and adults in their own home. Caregivers help with bathing, dressing, meals, light housekeeping, medication reminders, and companionship. Home care is different from home health care, which is medical and needs a doctor's order.
Home care costs vary by where you live and how many hours you need. The national average is $25 to $35 per hour. Live-in care usually runs $200 to $400 per day. Costs are higher in big cities and for memory care. Most agencies offer a free consultation to give you a real price estimate.
Medicare generally does not cover non-medical home care like bathing, meals, or companionship. Medicare does cover home health care (skilled nursing, physical therapy) if a doctor orders it. Medicaid may cover home care in some states. Other options are long-term care insurance and VA benefits for veterans.
Home care is non-medical help with daily living: bathing, meals, housekeeping, company. Home health care is medical: skilled nursing, wound care, physical therapy, ordered by a doctor. Home care helps someone stay independent. Home health care treats a medical problem.
Start with agencies that are licensed in your state and run background checks on caregivers. Ask about training, how they match caregivers to clients, what happens if your regular caregiver is out, and how they handle concerns. Ask for references. Meet at least one caregiver before care starts. Accreditation from the Joint Commission or CHAP is a good sign.
